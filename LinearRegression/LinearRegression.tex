Suppose we are given a dataset of $n$ observations

\[
\mathbf{X} =
\begin{bmatrix}
x_{11} & x_{12} & \cdots & x_{1p} \\
x_{21} & x_{22} & \cdots & x_{2p} \\
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
x_{n1} & x_{n2} & \cdots & x_{np}
\end{bmatrix}
\]

with the corresponding target values
\[
\mathbf{y} = 
\begin{bmatrix}
y_1 \\
y_2 \\
\vdots \\
y_n
\end{bmatrix}.
\]
\\
Linear Regression uses a linear function

\[
f(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x} + b,
\]

where $\mathbf{x}$ is the feature vector, $\mathbf{w}$ is the coefficient vector, and $b$ is the intercept. \\

For all observations, the predicted values can be written as
\[
\hat{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w} + b\mathbf{1},
\]
where $\hat{\mathbf{y}}$ is the vector of predictions and $\mathbf{1}$ is an $n$-dimensional vector of ones. \\

To simplify the notation, we incorporate the intercept term into the design matrix by appending a column of ones to $\mathbf{X}$. The augmented design matrix is

\[
\tilde{\mathbf{X}}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
x_{11} & x_{12} & \cdots & x_{1p} & 1 \\
x_{21} & x_{22} & \cdots & x_{2p} & 1 \\
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\
x_{n1} & x_{n2} & \cdots & x_{np} & 1
\end{bmatrix}.
\]

Similarly, we augment the coefficient vector as

\[
\tilde{\mathbf{w}}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
w_1 \\
w_2 \\
\vdots \\
w_p \\
b
\end{bmatrix}.
\]
For simplicity, we will continue to denote the augmented design matrix and coefficient vector by $\mathbf{X}$ and $\mathbf{w}$, respectively. Then the prediction function can then be written as

\[
\hat{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w}.
\]

The aim of Linear Regression is to minimize the following loss function
\[
L(\mathbf{w}) =
\|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w}\|^2.
\]

Equivalently, we seek the parameter vector that minimizes this loss:

\[
\hat{\mathbf{w}} = \operatorname*{argmin}_{\mathbf{w}}\|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w}\|^2.
\]

Expanding the objective function gives

\[
\|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w}\|^2 = (\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{X}\mathbf{w})^\top(\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{X}\mathbf{w}).
\]
Taking the derivative with respect to $\mathbf{w}$ and setting it equal to zero yields
\[
\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w} = \mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{y}.
\]

These are known as the normal equations. Assuming $\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X}$ is invertible, the solution is
\[
\hat{\mathbf{w}} = (\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{y}.
\]
